# Checkpoint 1: Understanding Molecular Dynamics, Interatomic Potentials, and Machine Learning Interatomic Potentials

## Writeup

Molecular Dynamics (MD) is a powerful computational simulation methodology that finds extensive application across diverse scientific and engineering domains, including materials science, chemistry, biophysics, and nanotechnology, by modeling the physical movements of atoms and molecules over time using the fundamental framework of Newtonian classical mechanics (Alder & Wainwright, 1959; Rahman, 1964). In a typical MD simulation, each atom in the system is treated as a classical point particle, and Newton's second law of motion (F = ma) is numerically integrated at extremely small discrete time steps—typically on the order of femtoseconds—to propagate the trajectories of all particles, thereby providing a dynamic picture of the system's evolution at the atomistic scale (Frenkel & Smit, 2002). The central quantity governing the simulation is the potential energy surface (PES), a mathematical function that describes the potential energy of the system as a function of all atomic positions; the negative gradient of this PES with respect to atomic coordinates yields the forces acting on each atom, which in turn dictate the accelerations, velocities, and subsequent positions through numerical integration schemes such as the Verlet or velocity-Verlet algorithms (Allen & Tildesley, 2017). This atomistic approach acts as a virtual laboratory that bridges microscopic interactions and macroscopic thermodynamic, structural, and mechanical properties, enabling researchers to compute quantities like lattice constants, cohesive energies, elastic moduli, phonon dispersion relations, thermal conductivity, and defect formation energies—many of which are difficult or impossible to measure experimentally under controlled conditions.

The accuracy and physical fidelity of any MD simulation fundamentally hinges on the quality of the interatomic potential (also referred to as a force field) used to describe the interactions between atoms, and the development of these potentials has been a cornerstone challenge of computational materials science for over half a century. Classical empirical interatomic potentials are mathematical functions with fixed analytical forms whose parameters are fitted to reproduce a target set of properties obtained from either quantum mechanical calculations—most commonly Density Functional Theory (DFT)—or experimental measurements such as lattice constants, elastic constants, cohesive energies, and phonon frequencies (Daw & Baskes, 1984; Tersoff, 1988). Among the most widely used classical potentials for covalent semiconductor systems like Silicon (Si) and Germanium (Ge) are the Tersoff potential and the ReaxFF reactive force field. The Tersoff potential, originally developed by Jerry Tersoff in the late 1980s (Tersoff, Phys. Rev. B 37, 6991, 1988; Tersoff, Phys. Rev. B 39, 5566, 1989), is a bond-order potential that goes beyond simple pair interactions by incorporating the local bonding environment into the calculation of interatomic forces: specifically, the strength of a bond between two atoms is modulated by a bond-order function that depends on the number and geometric arrangement of neighboring atoms, thereby capturing the directional character of covalent bonds that is essential for accurately describing diamond-cubic structures such as those adopted by Si and Ge. The Tersoff potential was subsequently extended to multicomponent systems including Si-Ge alloys by interpolating between elemental parameter sets to describe heteronuclear bonds, and it has been shown to reproduce ground-state lattice constants (Si: ~5.43 Å experimental; Ge: ~5.66 Å experimental) and cohesive energies with reasonable accuracy, though it can struggle with properties outside its fitting domain, such as high-temperature phase behavior, amorphous structures, and certain defect configurations. ReaxFF (Reactive Force Field), developed by van Duin, Goddard, and co-workers (van Duin et al., J. Phys. Chem. A 105, 9396, 2001), represents a more sophisticated class of empirical potential that was designed to model chemical reactions—specifically the continuous breaking and formation of chemical bonds—using a bond-order formalism where bond orders are dynamically calculated from interatomic distances at every simulation step, thereby allowing bonds to form and break smoothly without energy or force discontinuities. The total energy in ReaxFF is decomposed into various bonded contributions (bond, angle, torsion terms that depend on bond order) and non-bonded interactions (van der Waals and Coulombic terms computed with dynamically updated partial charges via the Electronegativity Equalization Method), and the entire parameter set is trained against extensive DFT datasets to reproduce reaction barriers, energies, and structural properties for specific element combinations. While ReaxFF enables the study of reactive processes in large systems (thousands to hundreds of thousands of atoms) at timescales inaccessible to ab initio methods, its accuracy is inherently limited by the fixed functional form and the quality of its parameterization for the specific chemistry under investigation.

However, classical interatomic potentials—despite their computational efficiency and decades of development—suffer from fundamental limitations: their accuracy is bounded by the assumed analytical functional form, they often require compromises between different material properties during parameter fitting, and they generally exhibit poor transferability to configurations or conditions not well represented in their training set. These limitations become particularly acute for elements that are difficult to synthesize naturally or exhibit exotic electronic properties, such as the Actinides and Lanthanides, where reliable experimental data for potential parameterization may be scarce or altogether unavailable. To address these challenges, the materials science and computational chemistry communities have in recent years increasingly turned to Machine Learning Interatomic Potentials (MLIPs), which represent a paradigm shift from hand-crafted analytical functions to flexible, data-driven models that learn the complex, high-dimensional potential energy surface directly from large datasets of ab initio (typically DFT) calculations (Behler & Parrinello, 2007; Bartók et al., 2010; Shapeev, 2016). Unlike classical potentials, MLIPs do not assume a fixed functional form; instead, they employ high-dimensional regression using neural networks, graph neural networks, kernel methods, or tensor decomposition frameworks to map atomic configurations to energies, forces, and stresses with near-DFT accuracy but at a computational cost that is orders of magnitude lower than direct DFT, scaling linearly with system size and enabling simulations of thousands to millions of atoms over nanosecond timescales.

Among the most prominent and widely adopted MLIPs in the current landscape are MACE, DeePMD-kit, and CHGNet, each of which embodies distinct architectural innovations and capabilities. MACE (Multilayer Atomic Cluster Expansion), introduced by Batatia et al. at NeurIPS 2022, is a state-of-the-art equivariant graph neural network framework that combines equivariant message passing with the Atomic Cluster Expansion (ACE) formalism to construct interatomic potentials with exceptional accuracy and computational efficiency. The key innovation of MACE lies in its ability to explicitly incorporate higher-body-order geometric features—going beyond pairwise interactions to capture many-body correlations—through its message-passing architecture, while simultaneously decoupling the model's expressivity from its depth, meaning that very high many-body order interactions can be achieved with as few as one or two message-passing layers, resulting in excellent computational efficiency, training speed, and parallelizability. MACE's representations are constructed using equivariant operations—transforming consistently under rotations and translations of the atomic system via spherical harmonics and irreducible representations of the rotation group—which ensures that the model rigorously respects the fundamental physical symmetries of the potential energy surface. Crucially, MACE has also been developed into a "foundation model" paradigm through MACE-MP-0, a pre-trained universal interatomic potential trained on the Materials Project Trajectory (MPtrj) dataset encompassing over 1.5 million inorganic structures and parameterized for 89 chemical elements across the periodic table; this pre-trained model provides reasonable out-of-the-box predictions for a vast range of materials—including Si and Ge—and can be fine-tuned on smaller, system-specific datasets for enhanced accuracy, making it an exceptionally versatile starting point for materials simulations. DeePMD-kit (Deep Potential Molecular Dynamics), developed by the DeepModeling community (Zhang et al., Phys. Rev. Lett. 120, 143001, 2018; Wang et al., Comput. Phys. Commun. 228, 178, 2018), is another major open-source framework for constructing deep learning-based interatomic potentials that bridges the accuracy-efficiency gap by training neural network models—termed "Deep Potentials"—on ab initio data to perform molecular dynamics simulations with near-quantum-mechanical accuracy at linear computational scaling. DeePMD-kit employs a descriptor-based approach where the local atomic environment is encoded through symmetry-preserving descriptors (such as DeepPot-SE, DPA-1, and DPA-2), and a neural network maps these descriptors to per-atom energies; the framework has evolved significantly with its v3 release, which introduced a multi-backend architecture supporting TensorFlow, PyTorch, JAX, and PaddlePaddle, as well as the DPA-2 Large Atomic Model designed for universal molecular and material simulations. DeePMD-kit integrates seamlessly with major MD simulation engines including LAMMPS, i-PI, AMBER, CP2K, GROMACS, and OpenMM, and its plugin mechanism (DeePMD-GNN) further enables the incorporation of external graph neural network architectures such as NequIP and MACE into its unified framework. CHGNet (Crystal Hamiltonian Graph Neural Network), introduced by Deng et al. (Nature Machine Intelligence 5, 1031, 2023), represents a distinctive advance in the MLIP landscape by explicitly incorporating magnetic moments—serving as proxies for electronic charge states and valence information—into a graph neural network framework, thereby enabling charge-informed atomistic modeling of inorganic crystalline materials. CHGNet was pre-trained on the Materials Project Trajectory Dataset containing over 1.5 million inorganic structures covering static and relaxation trajectories across a vast majority of the periodic table, and it can predict energies, forces, stresses, and magnetic moments with near-DFT accuracy; its E(3)-equivariant graph-based message-passing architecture ensures correct symmetry behavior, and its unique ability to capture the coupling between electronic charge states and ionic rearrangements makes it particularly powerful for studying systems where electronic degrees of freedom are important, such as ionic conductors, battery materials, and magnetically ordered compounds. Like MACE-MP-0, CHGNet's pre-trained model can be used directly for rapid screening and can be fine-tuned for improved accuracy on specific systems of interest.

For benchmarking these potentials on Silicon and Germanium—both of which adopt the diamond cubic crystal structure (space group Fd-3m) and are prototypical covalent semiconductors—the key properties of interest include the equilibrium lattice constant (experimental values: Si ≈ 5.431 Å, Ge ≈ 5.658 Å), the cohesive energy (experimental values: Si ≈ -4.63 eV/atom, Ge ≈ -3.85 eV/atom), and the internal atomic forces (which should vanish at equilibrium in a perfect crystal but are critically important for evaluating potential accuracy during dynamics, near defects, and in non-equilibrium configurations). Reference data for these properties can be obtained from established databases such as the Materials Project (mp-149 for Si, mp-32 for Ge), which provides DFT-calculated lattice parameters and formation energies computed using the GGA-PBE exchange-correlation functional, as well as from the Open Quantum Materials Database (OQMD) and experimental literature. The comparison of predictions from the five potentials—Tersoff, ReaxFF, MACE (including pre-trained MACE-MP-0), DeePMD, and CHGNet (including pre-trained models)—against these DFT and experimental reference values will be performed using the LAMMPS (Large-scale Atomic/Molecular Massively Parallel Simulator) molecular dynamics engine (Thompson et al., Comput. Phys. Commun. 271, 108171, 2022), which provides native support for Tersoff and ReaxFF potentials as well as interfaces for deploying MLIP models, and visualization of simulation trajectories will be performed using OVITO (Stukowski, Modelling Simul. Mater. Sci. Eng. 18, 015012, 2010). The ultimate goal is to produce parity plots comparing predicted versus reference values for lattice constant, energy, and forces across all potentials and for both Si and Ge, thereby establishing which classical interatomic potential and which MLIP best reproduce the ground-truth data and providing a quantitative assessment of the relative merits of traditional empirical potentials versus modern machine-learning approaches in the context of these fundamental semiconductor materials.
